# RLCSD: Reinforcement Learning with Contrastive On-Policy Self-Distillation

- **arXiv:** [2606.11709](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.11709) (COLM 2026 submission)
- **Authors:** Leyi Pan, Shuchang Tao, Yunpeng Zhai, Lingzhe Zhang, Zhaoyang Liu, Bolin Ding, Aiwei Liu, Lijie Wen (Tsinghua University; Tongyi Lab, Alibaba Group; Peking University)
- **Code:** [THU-BPM/RLCSD](https://github.com/THU-BPM/RLCSD)
- **Why it matters to us:** This is a direct critique-and-fix of **the family of methods our pipeline is built on**. It names **SDPO** (Hübotter et al., our `SDPOTrainer`) as a baseline, diagnoses *why* one-sided self-distillation drifts toward style instead of correctness, and proposes a contrastive remedy that **uses the failed rollouts** — which is exactly the signal we currently throw away (our `use_successful_as_teacher=True` distills *successes into failures* but never exploits the *content* of a failure as a counter-reference). It also reproduces, as a named failure mode, the **length collapse** we saw in iteration-01.

---

## TL;DR

On-policy self-distillation (OPSD) builds a dense per-token signal from the gap between a model conditioned on a **privileged hint** (a correct/reference solution) and the same model without it: `e_c,t = log π_T(y_t | x, y*_c, y_<t) − log π_S(y_t | x, y_<t)`. The paper shows this signal is **dominated by style tokens, not task tokens** — conditioning on a correct solution makes the teacher shorter, more assertive, and suppresses hedges, and that *correctness-orthogonal* shift (they call it **privilege-induced style drift**) swamps the actual reasoning signal (style-token mean |e_c| = 0.263 vs task-token mean 0.083, ~3×). The fix, **RLCSD**, runs the teacher on a **correct hint AND an incorrect hint under a byte-identical template** and subtracts: `e_ctr,t = e_c,t − e_w,t`. Because the style drift is shared across both branches, it **cancels**, leaving a signal concentrated on task-bearing tokens. That cleaned per-token signal is then used only to **modulate** the GRPO outcome advantage (not replace it). Result: stable training (no entropy/length blow-up, no length shrinkage) and consistent wins over GRPO, OPSD, SDPO, SRPO, RLSD on Qwen3-1.7B/4B/8B and Olmo-3-7B, with the **largest gains out-of-distribution**.

## The mechanism, step by step

1. **The privileged hint corrupts the signal with style, not correctness.** Partitioning the vocab into "task" tokens (numerals, operators, math symbols) vs "style" tokens (`Wait`, `Therefore`, `\n\n`), the per-token magnitude `|e_c|` is ~3× larger on style tokens than task tokens, with almost no overlap. The top-ranked tokens by `|e_c|` are stylistic markers; the actual math content is diluted. This is the same epistemic/style story as the Microsoft self-distillation post-mortem, framed from the *gradient signal* side.

2. **Two failure modes follow, and the paper reproduces both.** In their Qwen3-4B training curves: OPSD/SDPO/SRPO suffer **entropy explosion → training collapse** (abrupt length explosion, reward + val crash); RLSD instead suffers **premature response-length shrinkage** that caps long-horizon performance. (Our iteration-01 sits in the second bucket — mode-collapse to terse outputs.)

3. **Contrastive cancellation cleans the signal.** Feed the teacher a **positive hint** (a correct sibling rollout) and a **negative hint** (an incorrect sibling rollout) under an *identical* "Reference Solution … Correct final answer: …" template — the negative hint is deliberately mislabeled as correct so the teacher treats both as ground truth. Subtracting the two teacher–student gaps removes the shared stylistic shift; what survives reflects whether the reference's *content* points at the right answer. Re-running the task/style analysis on `|e_ctr|` shows the style–task gap shrinks markedly.

4. **Two refinements make the contrast stable.**
   - **K-marginalized negatives (K=4).** Incorrect solutions are heterogeneous (many error types); a single random negative hint often shares no error mode with the target rollout, so the negative branch loses its sign. Averaging teacher probabilities over **K independent negative hints** drops the wrong-sign rate from ~51% to <8%.
   - **Exclude the target rollout from its own hint pool.** Conditioning the teacher on a rollout as its *own* hint makes it pathologically over-confident (high-prob token fraction +~30%) and destroys the per-token discrimination. Draw hints only from *siblings* `G± \ {y}`.

5. **Verifier-anchored modulation, not replacement (the RLSD/MiMo lineage).** The cleaned `e_ctr,t` never sets the update *direction* — the GRPO outcome advantage `A_ORM` does. `e_ctr,t` is squashed `r_t = λ·tanh(e_ctr/τ)`, **masked** so only ~20–30% of high-signal tokens are modulated (`m_t = 1{|r_t|>δ}`), and added to `A_ORM` with a **sign-preserving clamp** so a token's contrastive nudge can never flip the verifier's verdict. A **two-path loss** averages modulated and unmodulated tokens *independently* so the ~25%-of-tokens modulated path isn't diluted into plain GRPO (η controls its weight). Hyperparameters: K=4, τ=0.02, λ=0.5, δ=0.02, η=1.0; teacher = a student snapshot refreshed every **10 steps**.

## Key results

- **Beats every baseline** (GRPO, OPSD, SDPO, SRPO, RLSD) on almost every benchmark across four models. Gains over base on logical reasoning (Knights & Knaves): +10.9 / +6.8 / +14.4 / +9.9 avg for Qwen3-1.7B/4B/8B and Olmo-3-7B. Math (AMC23/AIME24/AIME25): +4.3 / +2.5 / +2.7 / +1.8 avg.
- **OOD is where it pays off most.** On the hardest held-out K&K splits (9/10/11 roles, *unseen* difficulty), RLCSD gains up to **+21.0** (Qwen3-8B, 11 roles) and **+20.0** (Qwen3-1.7B, 10 roles) — evidence the cleaned signal improves *generalization*, not just train-difficulty fit. Note: the baseline **SDPO frequently regressed below base** (e.g. Qwen3-1.7B math 51.9→49.6) — consistent with our experience.
- **Training stays stable.** RLCSD holds entropy and response length flat like GRPO, while SDPO/OPSD/SRPO blow up and RLSD shrinks.
- **The contrastive principle is a drop-in.** Adding "own correct + incorrect rollouts in contrast" as the hint source *improves OPSD and RLSD too* (e.g. RLSD math avg +2.2, RLCSD math avg +3.0 over their best non-contrastive hint) — so the idea transfers beyond their specific objective.
- **Ablations:** removing K-marginalization, adding the self-rollout back into the hint pool, removing `A_ORM` anchoring, or collapsing the two-path loss each costs ~1–3 points; `A_ORM` anchoring is the single most important component (math avg 78.0→75.7 without it).

## How this maps onto SparkyCoder (this is the important part)

Our trainer is **literally one of the baselines this paper beats**, and the specific thing it fixes — one-sided positive-only hints — is exactly our current config.

- **We are the one-sided-positive case it critiques.** `src/sdpo_train.py:134` sets `use_successful_as_teacher=True` with `success_reward_threshold=1.0`: the teacher is conditioned only on **successful** rollouts (or the solution, or judge feedback). The paper's whole thesis is that a *positive-only* privileged hint injects style drift, and that **the missing ingredient is the failed rollouts used as a contrastive negative**. We have an abundance of failed rollouts (most of OJBench is hard) and currently use them only as distillation *targets*, never as *negative references*.
- **It re-explains iteration-01's collapse from the signal side.** Our easy-only 100-step run mode-collapsed to terse outputs (held-out pass@k *and* GSM8K dropped) — the paper's **"premature response-length shrinkage"** failure mode (the RLSD curve). Their diagnosis: the privileged teacher's signal is dominated by style/length tokens, so the student learns "be short and assertive" rather than "be correct."
- **Our teacher is EMA — RLCSD uses a periodically-refreshed snapshot.** `sdpo_train.py:133` `teacher_model_kind="ema"`. RLCSD refreshes the teacher snapshot **every 10 steps** (a stale-fixed teacher, not a continuous EMA). This corroborates the companion finding (the Microsoft paper) that a moving/EMA teacher *amplifies* the collapse feedback loop; a periodically-frozen teacher is safer.
- **The 0-successful-rollout gotcha cuts both ways.** Our hard gotcha (easy+medium cold → 0 successes → no teacher) is exactly RLCSD's "discard groups where `G+` or `G-` is empty." RLCSD needs **both** a success and a failure in the group — so on all-fail groups it *also* has no signal (same as us), but on mixed groups it extracts strictly more than we do (it uses the failure too). Our `environment_feedback_only_without_solution` (`sdpo_train.py:141`) is our current patch for all-fail groups; RLCSD is the better patch for **mixed** groups.

### Concrete things to try / instrument

1. **Build the contrastive signal from our existing group rollouts (highest-value experiment).** We already sample a group per problem and judge each (`make_reward_func` / `judge_completion` in `src/sdpo_ojbench.py`), so we already have `G+` (AC) and `G-` (WA/TLE/RE) per problem on **mixed** groups. The change is: for a target failed rollout, condition the teacher once on a correct sibling and `K` times on incorrect siblings under an **identical** "reference solution" template, and use `e_ctr = e_c − mean_k e_w,k` instead of the raw positive-only gap. This is a `SDPOTrainer` subclass — exactly the kind of "trainer subclass" the CLAUDE.md design-doc rule says to document in `docs/design/`. **De-risk per the budget ladder:** unit-test the contrast math + template symmetry, then a GB10 smoke, then a Modal preflight before any long run.
2. **Code-domain task/style token split.** Their drift diagnostic partitions vocab into task (numerals/operators/math symbols) vs style (`Wait`,`Therefore`,`\n\n`). Our analogue: **task** = identifiers, operators, language keywords, brackets; **style** = prose/markdown/`\n\n`/hedges. Compute per-token `|e_c|` on a held-out batch and check whether our positive-only teacher is also style-dominated on **code** before investing in the full contrastive trainer — this is the cheap "is the disease even present here" probe.
3. **Verifier-anchored modulation matches our judge.** RLCSD keeps the **verifier** (= our judge) as the direction-setting anchor and uses the distillation signal only to modulate magnitude, with a sign-preserving clamp so a noisy per-token nudge can't flip an AC/WA verdict. Given "judge fidelity = reward fidelity" is one of our standing hazards, this anchoring discipline (`A_ORM` decides direction, `e_ctr` only scales it) is a safer default than pure distillation (`distillation_weight=1.0`, `sdpo_train.py:130`). The ablation shows anchoring is their single most important component.
4. **Switch the teacher from EMA to a step-refreshed snapshot.** Cheap single-knob change to `teacher_model_kind` (snapshot refreshed every N steps, à la RLCSD's 10), motivated by both this paper and the Microsoft post-mortem. Compare held-out pass@k vs the EMA run.
5. **Watch entropy + length as leading indicators, two-sided.** RLCSD's two failure signatures are **entropy/length explosion** (SDPO/OPSD/SRPO) and **length shrinkage** (RLSD) — our iter-01 was the shrinkage kind. We already log length per step; **also log policy entropy** and treat *either* a fast collapse *or* a blow-up as a kill signal under the "watch the first few steps" budget rule. The SDPO loss won't warn us (matches our "loss is not a quality signal").
6. **K-marginalize and exclude-self if/when we implement the contrast.** Their two refinements (K=4 negatives; never use a rollout as its own hint) are cheap and each worth ~1–3 points in their ablation. With our small mixed groups, `K` may be limited by how many failures a group has — instrument `|G-|` per group first.

### Caveats / where we differ

- **All their evidence is math + logic CoT on 7–8B Qwen3/Olmo with thinking mode on.** We run **Gemma-4-E2B on competitive programming** (C++/Python). Style drift may manifest differently in code (less hedging, more boilerplate), so **run probe #2 before building the full trainer** — don't assume the math token-split transfers.
- **They use full-parameter training on 8×H20; we use LoRA on the text tower only** (regex `.*language_model.*\.(q|k|v|o|gate|up|down)_proj$`). The contrastive signal is orthogonal to LoRA, but the extra teacher forward passes (1 positive + K negatives per modulated rollout) **multiply teacher inference cost** — on the GB10's already-tight memory (microbatch=1, vllm util 0.30) and known multi-sample hang, this is a real integration risk. Budget the preflight accordingly; this likely belongs on Modal (H200) from the start.
- **Their "verifier" is a cheap answer-match; ours is a code judge that can stall.** The contrastive method needs `G+`/`G-` partition per group, which we already produce — but it leans harder on judge throughput (now judging the same rollouts plus running the teacher K+1 times). The serial-judging stall hazard from iteration-03 applies; keep the parallel judge path.
- **RLCSD needs mixed groups; OJBench-hard may give too few `G+`.** On genuinely hard problems we may get all-fail groups where the contrast has no positive hint — same coverage wall as our 0-successful-rollout gotcha. This reinforces the **iteration-02 "learnability frontier"** plan (easy + sometimes-solvable medium): it is also what maximizes the fraction of *mixed* groups RLCSD can actually learn from.

## One-line lesson

One-sided self-distillation (our `use_successful_as_teacher`) leaks **style, not correctness**, into the per-token signal and drives the length collapse we already paid for; **contrasting a correct hint against the failed rollouts we already generate** cancels that drift — so the highest-value next experiment is a verifier-anchored, contrastive `SDPOTrainer` subclass that uses `G−` as a negative reference, gated behind a cheap code-domain "is style-drift even present" probe and the usual de-risk ladder.
